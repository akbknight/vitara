The problem

Generic apps were built
for someone else.

Rahul is 28. Software engineer. Born in India, lives in Washington DC. He wears an Apple Watch. His app tells him he's doing fine. His dad got Type 2 diabetes at 47. His uncle at 52. Rahul knows the risk is there — he just can't get any health app to acknowledge it.

The meal plan the app gives him is full of food he doesn't eat. The workout schedule assumes he sleeps at 10 PM and wakes at 6 AM. The nutrition advice was written for someone else.

"Rahul is not an edge case. He is the majority of the world's health app users."

Western defaults

Dal, jollof rice, sabzi, tortillas — they don't exist in mainstream app databases. You either can't find your food or get wildly inaccurate macros.

Location blindness

The same plan for Chennai, Chicago, and Cape Town. Local climate, food availability, and practical exercise options are invisible to every major app.

The 6 AM assumption

Every plan assumes you wake at 6 and sleep at 10. Night workers, late chronotypes, Ramadan observers, and shift workers don't exist in the model.

Why now

Three forces
converging.

1

LLMs make culturally intelligent coaching possible at scale

Generating a meal plan in Tamil Nadu requires different knowledge than one in Toronto. Large language models finally make context-aware coaching feasible without a human nutritionist for every user.

2

The cultural gap is widening, not closing

Smartphone adoption is global. Health app content is not. The majority of new users entering the health-tech market do not fit the Western dietary template apps were built around.

3

Metabolic risk creates genuine urgency

South Asian, West African, and Latino communities carry disproportionate metabolic risk — and disproportionately unhelpful tooling. The urgency is real and the gap is measurable.
