The problem

Generic apps were built
for someone else.

Rahul is 28. Software engineer. Born in India, lives in Washington DC. He wears an Apple Watch. His app tells him he's doing fine. His dad got Type 2 diabetes at 47. His uncle at 52. Rahul knows the risk is there — he just can't get any health app to acknowledge it.

The meal plan the app gives him is full of food he doesn't eat. The workout schedule assumes he sleeps at 10 PM and wakes at 6 AM. The nutrition advice was written for someone else.

Western defaults

Dal, jollof rice, sabzi, tortillas — they don't exist in mainstream app databases. You either can't find your food or get wildly inaccurate macros.

Location blindness

The same plan for Chennai, Chicago, and Cape Town. Local climate, food availability, and practical exercise options are invisible to every major app.

The 6 AM assumption

Every plan assumes you wake at 6 and sleep at 10. Night workers, late chronotypes, Ramadan observers, and shift workers don't exist in the model.

"Rahul is not an edge case. He is the majority of the world's health app users."

The market

A $14B market built
for the wrong person.

The global health app market is $14 billion today, projected to reach $50 billion by 2030. Almost all of it serves one demographic: Western, English-speaking users with Eurocentric dietary norms. The 8 million South Asian diaspora users in the US and UK alone are an afterthought — and no funded competitor has been built for them.

Why now

Three forces
converging.

1

LLMs make culturally intelligent coaching possible at scale

Generating a meal plan in Tamil Nadu requires different knowledge than one in Toronto. Large language models finally make context-aware coaching feasible without a human nutritionist for every user.

2

The cultural gap is widening, not closing

Smartphone adoption is global. Health app content is not. The majority of new users entering the health-tech market do not fit the Western dietary template apps were built around.

3

Metabolic risk creates genuine urgency

South Asian, West African, and Latino communities carry disproportionate metabolic risk — and disproportionately unhelpful tooling. The urgency is real and the gap is measurable.

Why not ads

Users share metabolic data, food logs, and health goals. Selling that to brands undermines the trust the product is built on.

Why not freemium

Cultural health coaching has real infrastructure cost. Freemium trains users to expect full features without paying for them.

Founder

Why I'm
building this.

My family has a history with Type 2 diabetes. My father, my uncle, my grandfather — a pattern that cuts across generations. Watching them get diet advice built around food they've never eaten, from doctors and apps alike, is where this started.

When I moved to the US for my MBA, I tried every health app I could find. MyFitnessPal. Noom. Lose It. Not one understood the food I grew up eating. Not one had dal in its database. Not one knew that South Asian cooking has a completely different relationship with carbohydrates, spices, and ghee than the Western diet these tools were built around.

I'm doing customer discovery before writing a single line of app code — because building the wrong thing is the most expensive mistake a startup can make. Twenty-five discovery interviews later, I know the gap is real.

VITARA is the app I wish had existed ten years ago. For me. For my parents. For the hundreds of millions of people whose food, bodies, and lives were never the design target for the health tech industry.
