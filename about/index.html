The story

I grew up in a household where food was culture. Dal makhani was comfort. Poha was Monday morning. Biryani was a celebration. These aren't just meals — they are the texture of how I was raised and how I experience nourishment.

When I started using health apps seriously, I ran into the same wall every time. The food database didn't know what methi thepla was. The workout schedule assumed I was asleep before midnight. The calorie target was built for someone with a completely different metabolic profile and risk background than mine.

I have a family history of type 2 diabetes. My grandfather, my father's older brother, two uncles. The research is clear that South Asian individuals carry disproportionate metabolic risk at lower BMI thresholds than Western populations. And yet every health app I used was calibrated for someone who doesn't share my risk profile, my food culture, or my body clock.

"The problem wasn't that I needed more willpower. The problem was that the tool was never built for me."

I pitched VITARA at American University's ITEC-617 course in 2026. The judge gave me her contact card. Professor Ulstrup pushed me toward customer discovery before building — advice I'm following. Nilu, an experienced founder, told me to focus on the single strongest selling point and make it excellent. That advice reshaped how I think about the product.

What I've learned

What the process
has taught me.

1

Discovery before building is not a detour — it is the work

Every startup founder wants to build. The instinct is to ship. Professor Ulstrup and every mentor I've spoken to agree: the biggest waste is building the wrong thing. VITARA will not have a line of production code until we have confirmed, through real interviews, that the problem is painful, the solution direction is right, and people will pay. That is not slow. That is correct.

2

One differentiator, executed well, beats ten features executed poorly

Nilu's advice was direct: focus on the single strongest selling point and make one thing excellent first. I had originally built a features list with six dimensions of personalization. I was proud of its completeness. That was the wrong frame. VITARA's differentiator is cultural and contextual adaptation — and every decision now flows from whether it makes that one thing better or dilutes it.

3

The beachhead is not the company — it is the proof point

Starting with South Asian diaspora users is a strategic choice, not a limitation. It is the community where the gap between what generic apps provide and what actually works is most visible, most measurable, and most acute. Once we prove the model here, we apply the same adaptive engine to every underserved community globally. The beachhead is a test, not a ceiling.

4

The company I want to build is bigger than a South Asian health app

VITARA's long-term vision is the infrastructure for culturally intelligent health coaching at global scale. The adaptive engine that learns South Asian food culture today is the same engine that will learn West African, Middle Eastern, Latino, and East Asian food cultures tomorrow. The vision is genuinely global. The execution starts local, because that is how you build something that actually works.

Where we are

Pre-product.
Customer discovery.

VITARA does not exist as a product yet. I am in the customer discovery phase — the phase where you learn before you build. Here is what I am doing right now:

25 discovery interviews — structured conversations with South Asian diaspora users in the US and UK. Three hypotheses to test. Not pitching. Listening.

Manual 8-week cohort program — 10 participants. WhatsApp check-ins. Culturally adapted meal plans built by hand. Real revenue before any code is written.

Business model validation — price sensitivity testing. WTP hypothesis: $12–15/month for a plan that actually fits. Must validate before building.

Follow-up from ITEC-617 pitch — the judge gave me a contact card. Staying in touch with mentors and investors who understand the problem space.

The rule Nilu gave me: run the cohort program before building the subscription app. If 8 of 10 cohort participants say they'd pay monthly to continue, the model is validated. Not before.
